Compound Interest

See how your account balances can grow with this interest type.



What is compound interest?

The definition of compound interest is basic: the interest that is earned on both the original financial amount and on the interest that is accumulating. Compound interest allows someone's savings to grow faster over time.

Accounts that use compound interest, such as a standard savings account, the return gets added to the original principal at the end of every compounding period, typically daily or monthly. Each time interest is calculated and added to the account, the result in a larger balance. With the compound interest formula, the account earns more interest in the next compounding period.


How Compound Interest Works




The formula to calculate the compound interest can also be seen as A = P(1 + r)n.
P is the principal (the initial amount borrowed or deposited).
r is the annual rate of interest (percentage).
n is the number of years the amount is deposited.
A is the financial amount accumulated after n years, including interest.

Pros and Cons of Compounding

Pros

  • Can contribute to building wealth long-term in savings and investments
  • Mitigates some wealth risks
  • Compounding can work when making loan repayments

Cons

  • Unfavorable for consumers making minimum payments on high-interest loans or credit card debts
  • Returns are taxable
  • Can be challenging to calculate


The Bottom Line

Compound interest maintains a chance to take advantage of the time value of money. Compound interest on savings and investments has a long-term effect, as this type grows deposits faster than the simple interest route. Overall, when choosing an investment, consider both the the number of periods and the interest rate, where a modest rate-of-return can accumulate rewards overtime.
